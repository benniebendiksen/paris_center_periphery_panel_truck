An Interactive Map Story

Paris:
Center & Periphery

A City of Privilege and Exclusion

By Lauretta · Vanessa Moussa-Adamo · Bennie · Freddly · Gaelle

Read On

Paris is often celebrated as the capital of romance, fashion, and luxury, but its history reveals a deeper story of division. From the medieval privileges of the Latin Quarter to the exclusion of the modern banlieues, the city has always been shaped by a tension between its glittering center and its periphery.

We begin with the Latin Quarter, where medieval educational privileges first established a sense of exclusivity, before moving to Haussmann’s transformation, which physically restructured the city to favor the elite. The story continues through the post-war era, exploring how immigrant labor rebuilt the capital while being pushed to the edges. Finally, we analyze transportation networks, showing how the Metro and RER solidified these boundaries rather than erasing them.

This project traces that history using interactive maps to explore how architecture, immigration, and transportation have drawn lines between those who belong in the center and those pushed to the edge.

Chapters

I

The Birth of the Latin Quarter

In medieval Paris, students established an intellectual enclave on the Left Bank. King Philip Augustus granted them exemption from taxes and the right to be judged only by ecclesiastical courts—distinguishing them from the common population.

By Vanessa Moussa-Adamo

II

Haussmann's Paris

Under Napoleon III, Paris was transformed: boulevards widened, squares beautified. The heart became saturated with culture—rebuilt to be admired by the elite, while peripheral areas remained underinvested and symbolically distanced.

By Freddly
Starts at Scene 5
III

Immigration and Reconstruction

After two World Wars, immigrants from Eastern Europe and North Africa rebuilt Paris. But housing was expensive in the center, so migrants settled in the banlieues—pushed aside, marginalized, and often targeted by police.

By Gaelle
Starts at Scene 9
IV

Transportation and Division

From its 1900 establishment, the Paris Metro sparked fears that linking Paris with industrial banlieues would destabilize the capital. The Metro served wealthier areas while the RER serviced immigrant-majority towns—questioning the égalité of these networks.

By Lauretta
Starts at Scene 14

About This Project
