Paris:
Center & Periphery

A City of Privilege and Exclusion

By Lauretta G DiangdiMoussavou · Gaelle G Cajoux · Freddly C JeanX · Vanessa L MoussaAdamo · Benjamin Bendiksen Gutierrez

Read On

Paris is often celebrated as the capital of romance, fashion, and luxury, but its history reveals a deeper story of division. From the medieval privileges of the Latin Quarter to the exclusion of the modern banlieues, the city has always been shaped by a tension between its glittering center and its periphery.

We begin with the Latin Quarter, where medieval educational privileges first established a sense of exclusivity, before moving to Haussmann's transformation, which physically restructured the city to favor the elite. The story continues through the post-war era, exploring how immigrant labor rebuilt the capital while being pushed to the edges. Finally, we analyze transportation networks, showing how the Metro and RER solidified these boundaries rather than erasing them.

This project traces that history using interactive maps to explore how architecture, immigration, and transportation have drawn lines between those who belong in the center and those pushed to the edge.

I

The Birth of the Latin Quarter

An 18th century map from 1705 depicts Le Quartier Latin—where the oldest colleges and universities in France are located. This area represented the intellectual and most elite groups of French society, where King Philip Augustus granted students exemption from taxes and the right to be judged only by ecclesiastical courts.

By Vanessa L MoussaAdamo
Scenes 2–6

In medieval Paris, the birth of the Latin Quarter was founded on the students' desire to separate themselves from the bourgeoisie and the center of political power, creating an intellectual enclave on the Left Bank, also known as the L’Université area in Paris.

This separation was formalized by a crucial jurisdictional privilege: after a violent riot, King Philip Augustus granted students and masters an exemption from taxes and military duties and—most importantly—guaranteed that they would only be judged by ecclesiastical courts, which were known not to apply the death penalty, thus distinguishing them from the Parisian bourgeoisie and common population.

This early academic freedom and its often-turbulent nature were later the target of satire, notably in the 16th century, where Gargantua ridicules the intellectual elite of the University. The university district is depicted as the center of a “foolish and easily excited populace”, gathering in sedition against the King's patience, with the sophists of the faculty chosen to engage in useless rhetoric.

However, later on it became the training grounds of the bourgeoisie, separating the masses by those who could afford to participate in education and those who could not.

The Map:

This is an 18th century map dating back to 1705. The map has a clear depiction of the Parisian arrondissements. More specifically, the Latin Quarter is the area where many of the oldest colleges and universities in France are located. This area represented the intellectual and most elite groups of French society. Today it is still an educational hub, recognized for its contributions to French culture.

II

Haussmann's Paris

Under Napoleon III, Paris was transformed: boulevards widened, squares beautified. The heart became saturated with culture—rebuilt to be admired by the elite, while peripheral areas remained underinvested and symbolically distanced. Baudelaire wrote of his melancholy watching this transformation.

By Freddly C JeanX
Scenes 7–12

This map represents much more than an aerial view of nineteenth-century Paris. It offers a crucial entry point for understanding how Paris developed during that time and up to the present day. We can clearly see the concentration of monuments, historical sites, cultural buildings, and centers of power in the heart of Paris. This phenomenon can be directly linked to Haussmann’s renovations of Paris carried out between 1853 and 1870, under the Second Empire during the reign of Napoleon III.

During this period, Paris was transformed rapidly through the widening of boulevards, the modernization of water and sewage networks, the beautification of squares, and the creation of straight avenues and monumental perspectives. These renovations shaped the Paris we know today, and this transformation pushed and encouraged Charles Baudelaire (1821–1867) to write. Baudelaire, a nineteenth-century French poet, reacted critically to these changes, which he associated with bourgeois dominance and what he called “modernity.”

Overwhelmed by melancholy and nostalgia, Baudelaire began writing after this transformation because he deeply hated the Parisian bourgeoisie and the effects of modernity on the city. In the poem “Le Cygne”, he writes:

« Paris change ! mais rien dans ma mélancolie
N'a bougé ! palais neufs, échafaudages, blocs,
Vieux faubourgs, tout pour moi devient allégorie
Et mes chers souvenirs sont plus lourds que des rocs ».

“Paris is changing, yet nothing in my melancholy has moved. New palaces, scaffolding, great blocks of stone, old neighborhoods—everything becomes an allegory, and my cherished memories weigh on me heavier than rock.”

The map perfectly illustrates this centralization. The heart of Paris appears as a space saturated with culture and economic development. This version of Paris was rebuilt to be admired, traversed, and inhabited by the elite bourgeois classes, unlike the peripheral zones.

In contrast, peripheral areas appear much emptier, almost decorative. This reveals how the spaces located beyond Paris’s limits remained underinvested, less developed, and symbolically placed at a distance—reserved for the lower classes, immigrants, and those “rejected” by society.

Haussmannian logic, one might admit, reinforces a socio-spatial separation. That separation would continue into the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. Even today, the Paris metropolitan region displays a sharp division between Paris—territory of upper-class professions, intellectual elites, high-value infrastructure, and everything prestigious—and the banlieue, where workers, employees, social housing, and a racialized population live.

From this, we can see how a system of segregation took hold and has become a contemporary reality.

III

Immigration and Reconstruction

After two World Wars, immigrants from Eastern Europe and North Africa rebuilt Paris. But housing was expensive in the center, so migrants settled in the banlieues—pushed aside, marginalized, watched, policed, and controlled. The myth of Paris contrasts sharply with the reality.

By Gaelle G Cajoux
Scenes 13–20

After World War I, France was devastated economically. Over 1.4 million soldiers disappeared, and millions were wounded. This major human loss created a shortage of workers. To help rebuild the country, France called on immigrants from Eastern Europe—specifically from Ukraine, Poland, and Italy. After World War II, the situation was similar. Many factories had shut down, the economy was weak, and France once again turned to immigrant labor. This time, workers arrived from North Africa—particularly Algeria, Morocco, and Tunisia—as well as from Sub-Saharan Africa.

Throughout history, Paris kept expanding, integrating nearby neighborhoods and former communes that were once part of the banlieues. These changes modified the city’s boundaries, but the arrondissements of the centre have remained at the heart of Paris. Since housing was expensive in the center, many of these migrants settled in the banlieues. This contributed to the idea that the banlieue is the area associated with immigrants and low-income individuals.

Salim Bachi describes how these immigrant workers, after rebuilding the city, were pushed aside when new technologies replaced them. Instead of being integrated, they’re marginalized and often targeted by police. And when racism comes into play, they’re racially profiled. In “Le Grand Frère” Bachi tells the story of an Algerian man living with his older brother in the banlieue of Paris. He feels lost in French society and struggles to blend in because of his origin. One day, while they were walking in the city, a police officer stopped them, accused them of stealing, and asked for their papers—an everyday experience for many young men in the banlieues.

Gaёl Faye reveals that same reality in Paris Métèque; the video clip ends with a Black man being followed by the police. This shows how racialized groups are often being watched, policed, and controlled.

That reality contrasts with the romanticization of Paris. The city is associated with the Eiffel Tower, the Louvre, Notre-Dame. For many, it’s the capital of high couture, fashion, luxury, and love. These tourist myths are engraved in the collective mind, and they contrast sharply with the everyday life of the city’s residents.

Many newcomers from Asia, the Americas, and Africa arrive with a dream of Paris that was “sold to them.” Once they’re there, they’re confronted with a society that puts them in a box based on origin, skin color, social class, and zip code. They feel compelled to blend in with the environment they’re conditioned to live in. Wherever they’re from, they share the same experience upon arrival: they’re “foreigners,” and don’t fit the phenotype associated with a “real” Parisian.

Immigrants rebuilt Paris, yet they’re pushed aside to its banlieues, physically and socially. This divide between the center and the periphery reveals how deep social inequalities and immigration have shaped the geography of the city.

IV

Transportation and Division

From its 1900 establishment, the Paris Metro sparked fears that linking Paris with industrial banlieues would destabilize the capital. The Metro served wealthier areas while the RER serviced immigrant-majority towns—questioning the égalité of these networks.

By Lauretta G DiangdiMoussavou
Scenes 21–29

From its establishment in 1900, the Paris Metro has been subject to many criticisms and concerns—particularly from “Parisians inhabiting the wealthy core of the city,” who, from the start, “worried that any metropolitan rail system linking Paris with ‘industrial suburbs’ or banlieues would destabilize the capital” (Sununu). Such fears upheld the physical and socioeconomic division between the center of Paris and its periphery and would bleed into the future structures of the modern Metro and Réseau Express Régional (RER) systems.

Despite these worries, “so great was the worldwide recognition of the Paris Metro, its cultural and economic effects, and its associated art movements that it cemented Paris’ status as the cornerstone of French social life” (Sununu). This recognition cultivated a sense of pride amongst Parisians and created a pull that attracted those who would flock to Paris in search of opportunities.

An example of the celebration of Paris and its progress appears in paintings by revered artists Claude Monet and Édouard Manet. Through their art, they immortalized the station of Saint-Lazare, the very first train station predating the Metro that still represented the progress France was making on the world stage.

Paintings were not the only art form that captured the station’s significance. The Polish-French poet Guillaume Apollinaire (1880–1918) remarked on how Saint-Lazare became a shared space in his poem “Zone” (1913). The Metro stations became a meeting ground for the wealthy, the impoverished, and everyone in between as they navigated life in Paris: “Eyes filled with tears you look at those poor emigrants / They believe in God they pray the women nurse their children / Their smell fills the waiting room of the station Saint-Lazare.”

Howard University researcher Zeynep Onur provides insight into how the growing housing crisis—and the increase in immigration—came to be, especially during the “Glorious Thirty,” a thirty-year-long period of “remarkable economic growth between 1945 and 1975” (Onur). The prosperity of post-war Paris led to an “influx of migrants, both from rural areas within France and former French colonies.” As the Metro lines developed, migrants relied on these networks to travel in and out of the heart of Paris for work.

In 1977, the RER lines were established, reaching much further into the outskirts of Paris than the Metro had. The RER was meant to help integrate more citizens from the peripheries, but the fact that the Paris Metro primarily served wealthier areas while the RER serviced “immigrant-majority towns” (Onur) implies that the conditions under which people belonging to either group traveled were vastly different in quality.

This “begs the question of the level of égalité that these networks actually provide” (Sununu). The center–periphery divide therefore remains a fundamental part of Paris to this day.
